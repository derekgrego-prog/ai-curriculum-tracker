The RTFC Framework — Anatomy of a Strong Prompt

framework

Core Concepts
CONCEPT 01
Be a Director,
Not a Googler
Treat Claude like a capable new analyst who knows nothing about your firm. Give full context every time — role, task, format, constraints.
✗ weak
"Summarize this OM"
✓ strong
"You are an analyst at a CRE investment firm. Extract the following fields from this OM into a table: price, SF, NOI, cap rate, major tenants, lease expirations. Write 'not stated' for any missing field."
CONCEPT 02
Give Examples,
Not Just Instructions
Show Claude what good output looks like — don't just describe it. Claude will match the pattern of your example in structure, tone, length, and detail. Called few-shot prompting.
✗ weak
"Write a brief investment summary in our firm's style"
✓ strong
"Write a brief investment summary. Here is an example we liked: [paste prior example]. Match this tone, length, and structure exactly."
Rule of thumb: if you've written something similar before that you liked, paste it in. One good example beats three paragraphs of instructions.
CONCEPT 03
Tell Claude How
to Think
Ask Claude to reason step by step before giving a conclusion. Called chain-of-thought prompting. Forces errors to surface during reasoning rather than in the final answer.
✗ weak
"Is this a good value-add opportunity?"
✓ strong
"Assess this opportunity step by step: first occupancy vs. market, then unit upside by type, then NOI improvement potential, then overall assessment with key risks."
Shortcut: adding "think through this step by step before answering" to any prompt meaningfully improves output quality. The reasoning shown is useful in itself — you can spot errors or use the logic as a starting point.
CONCEPT 04
Iterate,
Don't Restart
Treat prompting as a conversation, not a single shot. Claude remembers everything earlier in the thread — build on what it produced rather than rewriting from scratch.
✗ weak
Rewriting the entire prompt from scratch when output isn't right
✓ strong
"Good — now make the exec summary two sentences shorter" / "Rewrite the risk section to focus specifically on lease rollover risk"
Mindset shift: your first prompt is a starting point, not a final spec. React to output rather than trying to anticipate everything upfront. Caveat: for very long sessions, start a fresh conversation to avoid context limits.
CONCEPT 05
Control the Output
Format Explicitly
Don't let Claude choose the format — specify it. Format and content are separate problems. Get format right upfront and output drops straight into your workflow.
✗ weak
"Summarize the key terms of this lease"
✓ strong
"Summarize key lease terms in a two-column table: Term | Detail. Rows: tenant, SF, commencement, expiration, base rent, escalations, renewal options, termination rights. Write 'not stated' for missing fields."
Key formats: table, bullets, numbered list, memo structure, prose paragraphs, JSON. Control length too: "in no more than three sentences" / "exactly five bullet points." Exclude formats: "do not use bullet points — write in full paragraphs."
CONCEPT 06
Know What Claude
Is Bad At
Confident use means knowing when to trust output and when to verify carefully.
✗ hallucination
Claude states specific figures, dates, or legal terms confidently — but incorrectly. Always instruct it to write "not stated" for missing info, and verify numbers against source docs.
✗ sycophancy
Claude will cave if you push back, even when it was right. Fix: "don't change your answer just because I'm pushing back — only update if I give you a good reason."
Also watch for: stale market data (no current comps or rates), long document degradation (accuracy drops late in large files), and math errors on complex calculations — set up logic in Claude, run numbers in Excel.

Rule: use freely for drafting, summarizing, structuring. Verify carefully when output involves specific numbers, legal terms, or anything going in front of your IC or LPs.
